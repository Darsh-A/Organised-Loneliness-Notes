
### Argument from Analogy
> The argument from analogy is a classical attempt to come to grips with the problem of intersubjectivity. The only mind I have direct access to is my own. My access to the mind of another is always mediated by his bodily behaviour by noticing the analogy that exists between this body and my own body, I infer that the foreign body is probably also linked in a similar manner to a foreign mind.

The traditional epistemological "problem of other minds" asks:
*How do i know you have a mind if i can only ever experience my own?*
The classical solution is **Argument from Analogy**. It relieson an analogical inference. I know that when i feel pain, I scream. When I observe your body screaming, I notice the physical similarity to my own behaviour, and I ***infer*** that you must possess a hidden, internal mind experiencing pain just like mine.

### Scheier's Critique of Analogy
>To assume that our belief in the existence of other minds is inferential in nature is to opt for a far too complex cognitive account. After all, both animals and infants seem to share this belief, but in their case it is hardly the result of a process of inference.

##### Cognitive Overload:
Infants and animals react to other minds immediately. They do not possess the complex cognitive capacity required to perform an analogical inference.
###### Asymmetry of Givenness:
How my body is given to me looks nothing like how your body is given to me. Thus no direct analogy can be drawn
###### Hidden Mind Fallacy:
The analogy argument wrongly assumes a Cartesian dualism where the mind is locked away in a private box and the body is a meaningless shell. Scheler argues that we perceive others as a unified whole. Anger is not a hidden mental state causing a clenched fist; the anger is directly manifest in the clenched fist. We have a direct access to the emotions of others through their expressive bodily behaviour.

### The Empathic Approach and Going Beyond It

> The model of empathy or the empathic approach rejects the idea that the relation between self and other is established by way of analogical inference, and instead argues for the existence of a specific mode of consciousness, called empathy, which is taken to allow us to experience and understand the feelings of others in a more-or-less direct manner.

The **Empathic Approach** solveds the problem of other minds by positioning ***empathy*** as a unique, irreducible form of intentionality. We dont infer the other; we directly empathise with th4em in face-to-face encounters.

However, Zahavia argues that phenomenology must go ***beyond empathy***, Because treating intersubjectivity merely as a concrete face-to-face, "thematic" exchange between two people is too narrow. The deepest phenomenological insights reveal that intersubjectivity is a foundational, a priori ontological structure of our existence, present even when no one else is physically in the room.
