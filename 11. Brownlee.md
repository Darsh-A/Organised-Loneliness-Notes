
# Social Contribution Injustice (SCI)

> To be human is to have a deep wish, indeed need, to contribute to other people’s survival and wellbeing. We wish to be someone on whom others can depend, and do depend, for care and companionship.

When political philosophers talk about "justice," they usually talk about the fair distribution of money, property, or legal rights. Brownlee argues this misses a massive part of human existence. We are fundamentally social creatures, and a core part of a flourishing human life is the ability not just to receive care, but to **give** care.

Brownlee introduces the concept of **Social Contribution Injustice (SCI)**. This injustice occurs when society denies an individual the right to be a "social contributor"—someone who gives love, advice, companionship, and support to others. To be a contributor, we require three core social resources: the **ability** to do so (cognitive/emotional skills), the **opportunities** to do so (access to people), and the actual **connections** (ongoing relationships). Stripping someone of these resources is a profound rights-violation.

# Two Forms of Social Contribution Injustice

Brownlee divides this injustice into two specific forms: one concerning objective resources (what we do to people), and one concerning societal attitudes (how we view people)

### SCI-1 (Compromising Resources)
SCI-1 happens objectively in the physical/social world. It occurs when we actively block someone from developing their social skills (e.g., by locking them away) or block them from accessing the people they could contribute to. It is the physical or structural removal of their social resources.

### SCI-2 (Prejudice)
SCI-2 is an attitudinal or epistemic injustice. It happens when society looks at a person and pre-judges them as "socially needy" or useless, assuming they have nothing valuable to offer.

- **Undervaluing:** Assuming the elderly, the impaired, or children cannot contribute to the emotional well-being of others. (Or, in her Hong Kong hospital example, the hospital staff undervaluing the father as a capable primary caregiver for his quarantined child).

- **Overvaluing:** Stereotyping a group by expecting too much social contribution from them (e.g., societal prejudices assuming women are naturally supposed to handle all emotional/caregiving labor in a household).

### The Ripple Effect: Indirect Victims
A key philosophical point Brownlee makes is that SCI does not happen in a vacuum. If you put a father in solitary confinement (SCI-1), you don't just wrong the father. You also wrong his child, who is robbed of the father's social contributions. If you prevent someone from being a friend, you steal a friend from the community. Therefore, social contribution injustice inherently creates **indirect victims**.


# Compromising Social Abilities
To make social contributions, we first need the **ability** to do so. Brownlee highlights cases where society unjustly prevents someone from either developing or maintaining these abilities.

### Developing Social Abilities (The Mistreated Child)
>Imagine a child, Chloe, who is abandoned, neglected, abused. Chloe receives inadequate care, intimacy, and socialisation in childhood, and, consequently, fails to develop fully the cognitive, physical, emotional, linguistic, and social abilities necessary to be a meaningful social contributor. (Brownlee, p. 4)

Neglect is an extreme form of SCI-1. Because Chloe is denied basic social interaction, she is robbed of the chance to learn how to care for others. This severely limits her autonomy and her future ability to offer love and companionship. It also involves SCI-2, as society fails to recognize the immense social contributions children make (e.g., caring for younger siblings, enriching family intimacy).

### Maintaining Social Abilities (The Lonely Person & Coercive Isolation)

> Lonely Person: Imagine that Maya has significant physical impairments and is unable to seek others company without assistance. She is thereby deprived of minimally adequate social contact and suffers the erosion of her social resources.
>Solitary Confinement: Imagine that Ali is held in coercive isolation. 
>Alone in Public: Imagine Beau is held in isolation against his will in a room with one-way mirror-windows.

Even if you developed social abilities in childhood, they can erode without use.

- **The Lonely Person:** If society neglects a physically impaired person, they commit SCI-1 (failing to assist her so she can contribute) and SCI-2 (prejudicially viewing her only as a "burden" to be cared for, rather than someone who can give care, empathy, and friendship back). This denies her the fundamental human need for **reciprocity**.
    
- **Coercive Isolation:** Putting someone in solitary confinement actively destroys their cognitive and emotional abilities. Brownlee argues this is an absolute human rights violation. Just as we cannot deprive a prisoner of oxygen or food as "punishment," we cannot deprive them of the basic human contact required to survive as a social being.


# Compromising Social Opportunities
Sometimes a person has their social abilities intact, but society denies them the **opportunity** to use them in a meaningful way.

### Lacking Authentic Opportunities 
***(False World & The Truman Show)***

Even if you feel like you are making social contributions, it is an injustice if the opportunity is fake. Truman lacks **associative control** and **authenticity**. He is severely wronged via SCI-2: he is simultaneously undervalued (treated as a prop/object for entertainment) and overvalued (millions of viewers are deeply invested in his life, but entirely in a one-sided, non-reciprocal way).

### Lacking Adequate Opportunities
***(Worst Enemies, Lack of Variety, Exile, & Shunning)***

- **Worst Enemies & Lack of Variety:** Being trapped with abusers or forced into the continuous company of a single person (like Sartre's No Exit or a prison cellmate) denies a person adequate variety and safe opportunities to express care and trust.
    
- **Exile & Shunning:** Physical exile cuts off pre-existing ties. Social shunning is fascinating because it isolates SCI-2 from SCI-1. If a society completely ignores a traveler (shunning), they aren't actively destroying her abilities (she can go home to her friends), but they are committing SCI-2: the society prejudicially refuses to view her as a potential social contributor, treating her as socially invisible.


# Compromising Social Connections

Finally, a person may have abilities and opportunities, but society unjustly prevents them from sustaining deep, continuous **connections**.

### Non-Repeated Contact
Julia has plenty of human contact, but it resets every day. Because she lacks continuity, she cannot build trust, share a joint narrative, or achieve intimacy. She is denied the ability to witness someone else's life over time, which is a core human social contribution.

### Severed Contact & The Denial of Grief
Allowing someone to form a bond and then using that bond as a weapon to hurt them by severing it is a profound injustice. Furthermore, prohibiting connections denies people the right to **grieve**. Grieving is not just a personal sadness; it is a vital **social contribution**. By grieving, we honor the value of the loved one and maintain our own capacity to be vulnerable and commit to others. Denying the space for grief destroys this social resource.
