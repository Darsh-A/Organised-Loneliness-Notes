
# Version 1

***Title: The Iron Band of Technology: Choosing Between Ontology and Politics in Modern Loneliness***

One of the biggest paradoxes of our time is that we are more connected by technology than ever before, yet we feel completely isolated. When trying to figure out why this is happening, philosophy gives us two main ways to look at loneliness. On one hand, Martin Heidegger argues that loneliness is a breakdown of our "relational ontology", it is a basic flaw in how human beings naturally exist with others. On the other hand, Hannah Arendt argues that mass loneliness is a politically produced condition forced upon us by oppressive systems. Diane Enns tries to combine these two views to explain the loneliness of the 21st century. However, I will argue that loneliness should primarily be understood as a politically produced condition (Arendt). While Enns accurately describes the modern world, her attempt to blend Heidegger’s and Arendt’s theories leaves her argument conceptually unstable because she confuses voluntary everyday choices with actual political terror.

To understand this debate, we first have to look at Heidegger’s idea of human existence in Being and Time. For Heidegger, human beings (Dasein) are not isolated minds trapped inside bodies. Instead, we are always "Being-in-the-world," and because we share this world, we are always "Being-with" (Mitsein) others.

However, just because we are always with others does not mean we actually feel like we belong. In our everyday lives, Heidegger says we naturally fall into the "they-self" (das Man). To make life easier and more comfortable, we just do what "they" do. We conform to social norms and let the crowd make our decisions for us. The problem is that this "leveling down" erases our unique individuality. We end up surrounded by people, yet completely hidden in the anonymous crowd. For Heidegger, this is an ontological breakdown. This type of loneliness is just a natural risk of human existence, and the only way out of it is to experience anxiety, face our own mortality, and take authentic responsibility for our own lives.

While Heidegger shows that human nature makes us vulnerable to loneliness, Arendt argues that the most dangerous kind of loneliness is a political weapon. For Arendt, human beings are defined by "natality" (the ability to start something new) and "plurality" (the fact that we are all unique equals). But these traits can only be expressed if we have a public "space of appearance", a political space where we can speak and act together.

In The Origins of Totalitarianism, Arendt shows what happens when this political space is destroyed. She makes a careful distinction between isolation and loneliness. Isolation just means you cannot act politically with others, but you still have your private life and friendships. Loneliness is much worse; it means you are completely deserted by all human companionship and lose trust in reality itself. Totalitarian regimes create this loneliness on purpose. They use the "iron band of terror" to press people so tightly together that their unique plurality is crushed into one giant, mindless mass. People become so desperate and disconnected that they stop thinking for themselves and surrender to the strict, "ice-cold" logic of ideology. For Arendt, this is not just a natural human flaw like Heidegger’s "they-self"; it is a condition actively imposed on people by a political system.

Diane Enns tries to bring Arendt’s political theory into the 21st century by arguing that we are currently living under a new form of "organized loneliness." Following Franco "Bifo" Berardi, Enns claims that today’s loneliness is not caused by dictators, but by "high-tech capitalism." Instead of an iron band of terror, we are controlled by an "iron band of technology."

Enns points out that human relationships have suffered an "anthropological mutation." We have moved away from "conjunction" (embodied, empathetic, face-to-face relationships) and replaced it with "connection" (swiping, liking, and following algorithmic rules). Because modern work is highly competitive and follows us everywhere on our phones, we lose shared physical spaces. As a result, people retreat into their nuclear families or totally withdraw from society (like the hikikomori in Japan). Enns argues that this proves organized loneliness is still happening today.

Enns’s description of modern society is very accurate, but philosophically, her argument is conceptually unstable. She tries to use Arendt’s political framework of totalitarianism, but the situation she describes actually fits much better with Heidegger’s ontological theory. The instability in Enns’s argument comes down to one word: choice.

Enns acknowledges that today’s 'iron band of technology' relies heavily on our own willing participation. We actively choose efficiency, convenience, and independence, ultimately accepting loneliness as the price we pay. But Arendt’s victims of totalitarianism did not choose to have their public spaces destroyed; they were forced into loneliness by the police, propaganda, and state violence. By contrast, when modern people choose to text instead of meet in person because it is easier, they are doing exactly what Heidegger described: falling into the comfortable, lazy habits of the 'they-self.' By trying to label our voluntary digital habits as a form of Arendtian 'organized loneliness,' Enns stretches the political concept too far and loses the strict definition of political terror."

Someone might object to this critique by bringing up Wendy Brown’s theory of neoliberalism (as discussed by Gaffney). Brown argues that neoliberalism is a political system that forces people to become isolated economic actors (homo oeconomicus). One could argue that under high-tech capitalism, we do not really have a choice; the economy forces us to be competitive, overworked, and glued to our screens just to survive, meaning our loneliness is politically imposed after all.

Even if we accept this objection, comparing the pressure of a capitalist job market to the literal terror of Nazi Germany or Stalinist Russia blurs the lines of Arendt’s theory too much. Neoliberalism might close off the public "space of appearance," but it does not use state violence to forbid people from talking to their neighbors. Enns says that to resist today's loneliness, we just need to accept being "interrupted or inconvenienced" by being good friends to people. But if the solution to a problem is simply being courteous and putting down our phones, then the problem is a personal, behavioral flaw (like Heidegger’s inauthenticity), not a totalizing political regime that requires a revolution to overthrow.

In conclusion, loneliness is best understood as a politically produced condition (Arendt) that takes advantage of our natural human vulnerabilities (Heidegger). Heidegger is right that human beings naturally look for easy, comfortable ways to fit in, which makes us prone to feeling anonymous. But it is Arendt who realizes the true danger: political systems can actively exploit this vulnerability to destroy human plurality. While Enns correctly points out the "desertification" of modern digital life, her theory remains conceptually unstable because she tries to apply a framework of political terror to a crisis of voluntary technological convenience. To fix the loneliness of the 21st century, we need to do more than just change our personal habits; we need to actively rebuild the public spaces of appearance where real, plural human beings can act together again.


# Version 2

One of the defining problems of our time is that we are constantly connected yet deeply isolated. When trying to make sense of this, philosophy usually points us in two different directions. Martin Heidegger treats loneliness as a structural part of human existence—an ontological vulnerability. Hannah Arendt, on the other hand, sees mass loneliness as a political weapon forced onto people by oppressive regimes. Modern thinkers like Diane Enns try to combine these two ideas to explain the digital loneliness of the 21st century. However, this paper will argue that loneliness is best understood primarily as a political condition (Arendt). While Enns hits the nail on the head when describing how modern society feels, her theory falls apart conceptually because she confuses voluntary, everyday choices with actual political terror.

To see why Enns’s argument struggles, we first need to unpack Heidegger’s view of human existence. For Heidegger, we aren't just isolated minds floating in space; our basic state is "Being-in-the-world." Because we share this world, our existence is always defined by "Being-with" (_Mitsein_) others `[Citation needed]`. We don't get a say in this; we are simply "thrown" (_Geworfenheit_) into a world of pre-existing social rules and contexts `[Citation needed]`.

But just because we are always with others doesn't mean we actually feel connected. Human beings carry the heavy burden of having to figure out their own lives, which causes a lot of anxiety. To escape this pressure, Heidegger says we have a natural tendency to "fall" (_Verfallen_) into the "they-self" (_das Man_) `[Citation needed]`. We just do what "they" do. We follow the crowd, adopt popular opinions, and slip into comfortable routines. This isn't a glitch or a breakdown in our nature; it's a built-in defense mechanism. In this state, we might be surrounded by people, but we are completely anonymous and alienated. For Heidegger, this kind of loneliness is an internal escape from responsibility, not something forced on us by a dictator.

Arendt sees things very differently. For her, the most dangerous kind of loneliness isn't a natural human coping mechanism—it's a deliberate political weapon. Arendt bases her theory on two ideas: "plurality" (we are all equal but totally unique) and "natality" (our ability to start something new) `[Citation needed]`. But we can only actually use these traits if we have a public "space of appearance"—a shared political space where we can interact and show who we are `[Citation needed]`.

When looking at totalitarianism, Arendt draws a hard line between isolation and loneliness. Isolation is bad—it means you can't act politically—but you still have your friends and private life. Loneliness is the complete destruction of both public and private life. Totalitarian regimes create this on purpose using an "iron band of terror." This force squeezes people so tightly together that their unique plurality is crushed, turning them into one giant, mindless mass `[Citation needed]`. Cut off from real human connections, people lose their grip on reality and blindly follow the cold logic of ideology. For Arendt, loneliness is a political tool built for domination.

Diane Enns tries to bring Arendt’s ideas into the present day. Drawing on thinkers like Franco Berardi, she argues that we are living through a new kind of "organized loneliness" driven by "high-tech capitalism" `[Citation needed]`. Instead of an iron band of terror, she says we are trapped by an "iron band of technology."

Enns points out that our relationships have changed. We've traded real, face-to-face empathy ("conjunction") for algorithmic, screen-based interactions ("connection") `[Citation needed]`. Because modern work is so demanding and competitive, we lose shared physical spaces and retreat into our private digital bubbles. Based on this, Enns claims that Arendt’s organized loneliness is still happening, just through our phones and laptops.

Enns does a great job describing what modern digital life feels like, but philosophically, mixing Arendt and Heidegger here just doesn't work. The main issue is agency. Enns is trying to use Arendt’s strict political model of totalitarianism to explain a cultural problem that actually looks exactly like Heidegger’s idea of the _they-self_.

The whole argument breaks down when you look at choice. Enns admits that our digital habits—choosing to text because it’s easier than calling, or doom-scrolling instead of going out—are mostly voluntary `[Citation needed]`. We actively choose this isolation because it’s convenient. This is exactly what Heidegger meant by "falling" into the lazy comfort of _das Man_. Arendt’s victims, on the other hand, didn't choose to lose their public spaces; they were terrorized by secret police and state violence. By trying to call our voluntary screen-time "organized loneliness," Enns stretches Arendt’s political concept until it loses its meaning.

Someone might push back on this by bringing up Wendy Brown’s theory of neoliberalism. You could argue that under modern high-tech capitalism, we don't actually have a choice. The system forces us to act like isolated economic machines (_homo oeconomicus_) just to survive `[Citation needed]`. If the economy forces us to be overworked and glued to our screens, then maybe our loneliness is a politically imposed condition after all.

Even if we agree that capitalism puts massive pressure on us, comparing a tough job market to the literal terror of Arendt's totalitarian regimes is a huge stretch. Economic pressure might make it harder to hang out in public, but the state isn't using violence to stop us from talking to our neighbors. You can see this gap in Enns’s own solutions. She says that to fix modern loneliness, we just need to be willing to be "interrupted or inconvenienced" by being good friends `[Citation needed]`. But if the cure to the problem is just having better manners and putting our phones down, then the problem is a personal flaw (Heidegger), not a totalizing political regime that we need to overthrow (Arendt).

Ultimately, mass loneliness really is a dangerous political weapon (Arendt), but it only works by exploiting our natural human flaws (Heidegger). Heidegger is right that we naturally look for the easy, anonymous comfort of the crowd. But Arendt realizes the true danger: bad political systems can use this vulnerability to destroy human plurality entirely. While Enns is right about how isolating the digital age is, her argument falls short because she treats a crisis of voluntary convenience like a regime of political terror. Fixing 21st-century loneliness takes more than just logging off; it requires actually rebuilding the public spaces where real, diverse people can act together.

# Version 3
**The Architecture of Isolation: Ontology, Politics, and the Instability of Modern Loneliness**

The modern era is defined by a strange contradiction: we are constantly connected through technology, yet we experience profound and widespread isolation. In trying to understand this crisis, two distinct philosophical frameworks emerge. Martin Heidegger treats loneliness primarily as an ontological breakdown, an inevitable hazard of our underlying existential structure. Hannah Arendt, conversely, frames loneliness as a politically produced condition, a weapon used to strip human beings of their plurality. In this paper, I argue that loneliness should be understood primarily through Arendt’s political lens. While Heidegger correctly identifies our structural vulnerability to alienation, it is Arendt who captures how specific political systems actively exploit this vulnerability to destroy human agency. Furthermore, while Diane Enns makes a compelling attempt to resolve the tension between these two thinkers by analyzing modern digital capitalism, her interpretation ultimately leaves the concept of loneliness unstable. By mixing up the chosen comforts of modern technology with the forced terror of totalitarianism, Enns accidentally reduces Arendt’s political argument to a basic Heideggerian ontological interpretation.

To understand why Arendt’s political framework is more effective, we must first examine Heidegger’s ontological baseline. For Heidegger, human existence (Dasein [1],[2] ) is fundamentally "being-in-the-world." Because we share this world, Dasein is also always "being-with" (Mitsein [3]). However, this native connection does not guarantee a sense of belonging. Heidegger argues that because we are "thrown" into a world we did not choose, we naturally seek familiarity and comfort to cope with our existence [1]. In doing so, we tend to surrender our individuality to the crowd, falling into the inauthentic mode of the "they-self" (das Man [1],[2]). In the "they-self," uniqueness is leveled down into averageness; we think what others think and judge how others judge.

For Heidegger, this is the root of modern loneliness: we are surrounded by others, yet we remain entirely hidden and anonymous. However, this framework is fundamentally pre-political. It describes the baseline architecture of existence. Because falling into das Man is an inevitable, structural pull of our being, Heidegger's loneliness is an ontological default rather than a deliberate political strategy. It falls outside the realm of politics because it is baked into our nature long before we ever step into public life.

Arendt recognizes the limits of treating loneliness merely as an existential default. She shifts the focus from ontology to the political sphere. For Arendt, human beings are defined by "natality" ([4] p7), the capacity to begin something new and "plurality" ([4] p7), the fact that we are all equal but entirely distinct. These qualities require a public "space of appearance" ([4] p7) to be realized. We only truly exist as free individuals when we can speak and act in concert with others in the public realm.

Therefore, for Arendt, loneliness is an active, politically produced condition. In The Origins of Totalitarianism, she outlines how regimes use terror to destroy the public sphere. By eliminating the spaces where people can act together, regimes strip individuals of their plurality. Terror acts as an "iron band" ([5],[6]) that destroys the space between people, pressing them together until their distinctiveness disappears into "One Man of gigantic dimensions" ([5] p.466). Arendt calls this "organized loneliness" ([5] p.478,[6]). It is the feeling of being completely uprooted and deserted by humanity. While Heidegger viewed the destruction of individuality as a passive byproduct of everyday socializing, Arendt rightly identifies it as a deliberate political mechanism designed to make populations totally susceptible to the coercive logic of ideology.

Enns attempts to resolve the tension between Heidegger’s ontological focus and Arendt’s political focus by building a strong argument that modern loneliness is no longer produced by totalitarian terror, but by "high-tech capitalism" [7]. To do this, she relies on Franco Berardi’s concept of the "anthropological mutation" from a culture of human empathy to a digital infosphere [7]. Enns unpacks Berardi’s crucial distinction between "conjunction" and "connection" [7]. Conjunction is the authentic mode of human relation: it is embodied, empathetic, and sensitive to nuance and ambiguity. Connection, by contrast, is the digital mode: it is functional, logical, algorithmic, and entirely devoid of empathy.

By framing modern life through this lens, Enns effectively channels Heidegger’s philosophy of technology specifically the idea of Gestell (enframing). High-tech capitalism "enframes" human relationships, turning human empathy into a collection of data, metrics, and useful but meaningless interactions. Enns argues that this technological enframing functions exactly like Arendt's iron band. It systematically produces a new "organized loneliness" by destroying shared physical workplaces and communal spaces. In this light, Enns presents a resolution that Heidegger’s critique of technology to explain how Arendt’s political isolation is being executed today without the need for dictators.

However, upon critical evaluation, Enns’s interpretation leaves the concept of loneliness theoretically unstable because it ignores the fundamental issue of coercion. Arendt’s concept of organized loneliness is strictly tied to forced terror; the loss of the public sphere is violently imposed from the top down. Enns, however, admits that the modern "iron band of technology" operates through consent. **As she writes, "Instead of fear, pleasure and convenience draw us into submission. We willingly trade our privacy and individuality for recognition and ease" (Enns, Organized Loneliness).**

This creates a severe structural gap. If modern individuals are willingly retreating from the public sphere because they prefer the convenience of digital "connection" over the demanding, vulnerable reality of human "conjunction," this aligns much closer with Heidegger’s concept of the "they-self" than Arendt’s totalitarianism. We are voluntarily leveling down our own uniqueness to participate in the comfortable world of social media. By equating the lethal, systemic terror of a totalitarian regime with the isolating effects of remote work and smartphones, Enns drains Arendt’s political critique of its original urgency. She inadvertently reduces Arendt’s political nightmare back into a Heideggerian ontological mood—a general, self-inflicted alienation of the modern era.

Yet, taking a firm stance on Arendt’s side requires us to reflect honestly on the limits of her framework today. If Arendt is right that loneliness is the political destruction of freedom's space, but Enns is right that capitalism has structurally erased these public spaces without using literal terror, then the Arendtian framework may lack the tools to fix the very problem it diagnoses. Arendt’s solution to loneliness demands that we step into the public "space of appearance" to act and speak with strangers. But if high-tech capitalism has permanently replaced the physical public square with digital echo chambers, Arendt’s demand for embodied political action might be practically impossible for the modern subject. Her framework perfectly diagnoses the political loss, but it struggles to offer a cure in a system where isolation is not only structural, but highly comfortable.

Ultimately, loneliness is best understood through Arendt’s framework as a politically produced condition that destroys human plurality. Heidegger provides an accurate map of human psychology, showing how easily we slip into anonymous conformity to avoid the anxiety of existence. But it is Arendt who shows what happens when that vulnerability is actively exploited by political structures to eliminate human freedom. While Enns provides a brilliant description of how modern technology isolates us, her attempt to label this as "organized loneliness" fails to reconcile the difference between the terror of being stripped of your humanity and the quiet tragedy of trading it away for convenience.
