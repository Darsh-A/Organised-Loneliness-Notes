***Title: The Iron Band of Technology: Choosing Between Ontology and Politics in Modern Loneliness***

One of the biggest paradoxes of our time is that we are more connected by technology than ever before, yet we feel completely isolated. When trying to figure out why this is happening, philosophy gives us two main ways to look at loneliness. On one hand, Martin Heidegger argues that loneliness is a breakdown of our "relational ontology", it is a basic flaw in how human beings naturally exist with others. On the other hand, Hannah Arendt argues that mass loneliness is a politically produced condition forced upon us by oppressive systems. Diane Enns tries to combine these two views to explain the loneliness of the 21st century. However, I will argue that loneliness should primarily be understood as a politically produced condition (Arendt). While Enns accurately describes the modern world, her attempt to blend Heidegger’s and Arendt’s theories leaves her argument conceptually unstable because she confuses voluntary everyday choices with actual political terror.

To understand this debate, we first have to look at Heidegger’s idea of human existence in Being and Time. For Heidegger, human beings (Dasein) are not isolated minds trapped inside bodies. Instead, we are always "Being-in-the-world," and because we share this world, we are always "Being-with" (Mitsein) others.

However, just because we are always with others does not mean we actually feel like we belong. In our everyday lives, Heidegger says we naturally fall into the "they-self" (das Man). To make life easier and more comfortable, we just do what "they" do. We conform to social norms and let the crowd make our decisions for us. The problem is that this "leveling down" erases our unique individuality. We end up surrounded by people, yet completely hidden in the anonymous crowd. For Heidegger, this is an ontological breakdown. This type of loneliness is just a natural risk of human existence, and the only way out of it is to experience anxiety, face our own mortality, and take authentic responsibility for our own lives.

While Heidegger shows that human nature makes us vulnerable to loneliness, Arendt argues that the most dangerous kind of loneliness is a political weapon. For Arendt, human beings are defined by "natality" (the ability to start something new) and "plurality" (the fact that we are all unique equals). But these traits can only be expressed if we have a public "space of appearance", a political space where we can speak and act together.

In The Origins of Totalitarianism, Arendt shows what happens when this political space is destroyed. She makes a careful distinction between isolation and loneliness. Isolation just means you cannot act politically with others, but you still have your private life and friendships. Loneliness is much worse; it means you are completely deserted by all human companionship and lose trust in reality itself. Totalitarian regimes create this loneliness on purpose. They use the "iron band of terror" to press people so tightly together that their unique plurality is crushed into one giant, mindless mass. People become so desperate and disconnected that they stop thinking for themselves and surrender to the strict, "ice-cold" logic of ideology. For Arendt, this is not just a natural human flaw like Heidegger’s "they-self"; it is a condition actively imposed on people by a political system.

Diane Enns tries to bring Arendt’s political theory into the 21st century by arguing that we are currently living under a new form of "organized loneliness." Following Franco "Bifo" Berardi, Enns claims that today’s loneliness is not caused by dictators, but by "high-tech capitalism." Instead of an iron band of terror, we are controlled by an "iron band of technology."

Enns points out that human relationships have suffered an "anthropological mutation." We have moved away from "conjunction" (embodied, empathetic, face-to-face relationships) and replaced it with "connection" (swiping, liking, and following algorithmic rules). Because modern work is highly competitive and follows us everywhere on our phones, we lose shared physical spaces. As a result, people retreat into their nuclear families or totally withdraw from society (like the hikikomori in Japan). Enns argues that this proves organized loneliness is still happening today.

Enns’s description of modern society is very accurate, but philosophically, her argument is conceptually unstable. She tries to use Arendt’s political framework of totalitarianism, but the situation she describes actually fits much better with Heidegger’s ontological theory. The instability in Enns’s argument comes down to one word: choice.

Enns acknowledges that today’s 'iron band of technology' relies heavily on our own willing participation. We actively choose efficiency, convenience, and independence, ultimately accepting loneliness as the price we pay. But Arendt’s victims of totalitarianism did not choose to have their public spaces destroyed; they were forced into loneliness by the police, propaganda, and state violence. By contrast, when modern people choose to text instead of meet in person because it is easier, they are doing exactly what Heidegger described: falling into the comfortable, lazy habits of the 'they-self.' By trying to label our voluntary digital habits as a form of Arendtian 'organized loneliness,' Enns stretches the political concept too far and loses the strict definition of political terror."

Someone might object to this critique by bringing up Wendy Brown’s theory of neoliberalism (as discussed by Gaffney). Brown argues that neoliberalism is a political system that forces people to become isolated economic actors (homo oeconomicus). One could argue that under high-tech capitalism, we do not really have a choice; the economy forces us to be competitive, overworked, and glued to our screens just to survive, meaning our loneliness is politically imposed after all.

Even if we accept this objection, comparing the pressure of a capitalist job market to the literal terror of Nazi Germany or Stalinist Russia blurs the lines of Arendt’s theory too much. Neoliberalism might close off the public "space of appearance," but it does not use state violence to forbid people from talking to their neighbors. Enns says that to resist today's loneliness, we just need to accept being "interrupted or inconvenienced" by being good friends to people. But if the solution to a problem is simply being courteous and putting down our phones, then the problem is a personal, behavioral flaw (like Heidegger’s inauthenticity), not a totalizing political regime that requires a revolution to overthrow.

In conclusion, loneliness is best understood as a politically produced condition (Arendt) that takes advantage of our natural human vulnerabilities (Heidegger). Heidegger is right that human beings naturally look for easy, comfortable ways to fit in, which makes us prone to feeling anonymous. But it is Arendt who realizes the true danger: political systems can actively exploit this vulnerability to destroy human plurality. While Enns correctly points out the "desertification" of modern digital life, her theory remains conceptually unstable because she tries to apply a framework of political terror to a crisis of voluntary technological convenience. To fix the loneliness of the 21st century, we need to do more than just change our personal habits; we need to actively rebuild the public spaces of appearance where real, plural human beings can act together again.
