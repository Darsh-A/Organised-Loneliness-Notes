
# Conception of Solitude
Gallagher and Janz begin by contrasting two historically opposed views of solitude.

- **Positive Solitude:** Religious and philosophical traditions often praise solitude. For thinkers like Wittgenstein (who withdrew to a remote cabin in Norway), solitude is an escape from the "crowd" that facilitates deep thinking, reflection, and an authentic "nearness to world."
    
- **Negative Solitude:** Conversely, psychology often views being alone as a precursor to depression and "social death" (either through total isolation or through becoming an anonymous nobody in a bureaucracy).
    
#### The Role of Control/Autonomy
What determines whether solitude is a "healthy aloneness" or a destructive "social death"? The authors point to **control** (autonomy). They cite Schulz’s retirement home study (where elderly patients who controlled when they had visitors felt less lonely) and studies of romantic breakups (where the person who initiates the breakup suffers less distress than the person rejected). The objective fact of being alone is the same in both scenarios, but **having agency** over the isolation dictates whether it is experienced as beneficial solitude or harmful loneliness.


# The Pattern Theory of Self (PTS)

To explain exactly how isolation harms us, the authors need a specific philosophical definition of what a "self" actually is.

The authors reject **"deflationary" theories** of the self. These are theories that reduce the self to just one single element—for example, neuroscientists saying the self is just an illusion created by brain neurons, or psychologists saying the self is nothing but the stories we tell about ourselves.

Instead, they propose the **Pattern Theory of Self (PTS)**. The self is a complex, dynamic web (a gestalt) made of many different factors. You are your body, your feelings, your habits, your physical belongings (extended factors), your social norms, and your relationships with others (intersubjective factors).

Because these factors are **dynamically interrelated**, changing one factor drastically alters the whole pattern. If you suffer from major depression, it doesn't just affect your mood; it changes your bodily posture, your memories, and how you relate to others.

#### The Extreme Case of Self-Dissolution (Solitary Confinement)

If the Cartesian view of the self were true (that the mind is a completely independent, isolated substance), then solitary confinement should be a great place to reflect and "find yourself." But phenomenologically, prisoners in solitary confinement undergo **derealization** (losing their grip on reality) and **self-dissolution** (losing their identity).

Why does a person "lose" themselves when confined to themselves? Because, according to PTS, the self is heavily constituted by **intersubjective relations**. When you forcefully sever the social factor of the self-pattern, the entire structure collapses. The mind literally becomes "unhinged" because the self is inherently relational.


# Affordance-Based Autonomy
To understand how solitude affects us, the authors argue we must redefine **autonomy**.

- **Relational Autonomy vs. Kantian Autonomy:** Traditionally (e.g., in Kant), autonomy is seen as pure self-sufficiency and rational self-legislation inside one's own head. Gallagher and Janz argue this is false. Autonomy is **relational**, meaning it is entirely dependent on our physical and social environment.

- **Affordances:** To measure autonomy, we look at **affordances** (the possibilities for action that the environment offers us). If the environment offers many things you can do, you have high autonomy. If it offers nothing, you have low autonomy.
 
- **The 3D Model of Affordances:** A person's autonomy can be mapped in a three-dimensional field:

    1. **Width:** How many choices/options do you have right now?
    2. **Depth:** How far into the future can you plan your actions?
    3. **Height (Affective Allure):** How much do you care about these options? Are they highly attractive or highly dangerous?

If a person falls into severe depression, this field shrinks: the "width" disappears (no options seem available), the "depth" vanishes (no future is perceived), and the "height" flattens (nothing is attractive). Their autonomy is severely diminished.

# Solitude Revisited

The authors use the concepts of **PTS** and **Affordances** to finally solve the paradox of solitude: Why does solitary confinement destroy the self, while a philosopher's isolated cabin enriches it?

- **Involuntary Isolation (Solitary Confinement):** When you are locked in a box against your will, your affordance space violently collapses. You lose all social affordances (no one to talk to) and physical affordances (nowhere to go). Because your autonomy drops to near zero and your social relations are severed, your **self-pattern disorders** and your identity dissolves (derealization, hallucinations, "social death").

- **Voluntary Isolation (The Philosopher/Mystic):** When Wittgenstein went to an isolated cabin in Norway, he chose to shrink his social affordances. However, this was a **trade**. By cutting out social distractions, he drastically increased the "height" and "depth" of his **intellectual affordances**. The mystic trades the physical affordances of the city for the spiritual affordances of the divine. Because this is an active choice that facilitates a deeply valued project, the person's **autonomy is maintained or even increased**. Therefore, the self-pattern remains intact and healthy.
